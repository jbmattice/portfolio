Accessible Course Catalog and Transportation Finder

Accessibility rebuild • Mobile-first • Section 508 / WCAG 2.1 AA

Modernized and optimized legacy content into a mobile-responsive, 508-compliant platform. The work focused on making course and transportation information usable for students and parents across devices and assistive technologies.

Key Insight: Accessibility issues often stem from structural constraints inside the system, not just visual design choices.

Academy Transportation Finder (opens in new tab)

Challenges
  • Legacy content was not designed for accessibility and required substantial restructuring
  • A mandatory CMS shell introduced layout and styling constraints that limited direct fixes
  • Known accessibility defects existed in the underlying structure and brand styling
  • The experience needed to work reliably on mobile, desktop, and with assistive technologies
Solutions
  • Re-architected the content using semantic HTML to improve structure and assistive technology support
  • Engineered custom HTML5/CSS3 workarounds to compensate for shell-level accessibility defects
  • Remediated layout, navigation, contrast, focus, zoom, and reflow issues
  • Added print support with a dedicated print stylesheet for users who needed hard copies
  • Made incremental improvements over a three-month period while working within existing constraints
Tradeoffs & Decisions

I could not change the CMS shell or brand styling directly, so I prioritized accessibility first and then improved usability with targeted enhancements such as print functionality and CSS refinements. If I had access to the shell CSS, I would have resolved more issues at the source; instead, I focused on the highest-impact changes that could be delivered safely within the existing system.

Impact: Students and parents now have full access to the information using screen readers, keyboard navigation, and zoom up to 400%. The experience is also more usable on mobile and desktop, with clearer structure, better performance, and improved print output.

Scalable Accessible Curriculum Timeline System

Data visualization • System design • WCAG compliance

Designed and implemented a reusable, accessible timeline system across 100+ curriculum pages by grade level and subject area to visualize learning progression by quarter and week.

Key Insight: Accessibility and translation needs shaped the information design as much as the visual presentation.

Grade 5 Science Curriculum↗ (opens in new tab)

Challenges
  • Organizational leadership needed a visual representation of the school year by week and quarter.
  • The source content was provided in a table, but a traditional table would not work because the data required merged and split cells.
  • Image-based solutions would not translate cleanly for families who needed multilingual access.
  • Many attempted solutions did not mesh with the CMS, requiring multiple iterations to find a workable CSS-only approach.
  • Unit lengths varied significantly, from 3-week units to 5-week units, and the design needed to adapt across grade levels and subject areas.
  • The solution had to be manually replicated across 100+ pages, so it needed to be scalable and efficient to maintain.
Solutions
  • Rejected image-based approaches so the content would remain accessible and translatable.
  • Avoided traditional tables because split and merged cells would create structural and accessibility problems.
  • Researched and tested CSS-only chart patterns until I found a solution that worked within the CMS.
  • Designed a flexible structure that could support varying unit lengths and differences across subjects and grade levels.
  • Created custom CSS that transformed the timeline into a mobile-friendly bulleted list on smaller screens.
  • Ensured the final pattern remained usable with keyboard navigation and screen readers.
Tradeoffs & Decisions

I needed a solution that balanced accessibility, translation, and maintainability while working within CMS constraints. I chose a CSS-only pattern because it could scale across 100+ pages, support mobile users, and adapt to changing content structures without introducing unnecessary complexity.

Impact: Teachers now share these pages with parents at the beginning of the school year during back-to-school night. Instead of relying on high-level PDFs or handouts, parents can see the exact standards taught in each unit by week and by quarter. This gives families a clearer understanding of the curriculum and improves communication between home and school.

Accessibility Audit & Remediation Guidance

WCAG audit • Developer collaboration • Remediation guidance

Conducted a comprehensive WCAG 2.2 AA audit of a dynamic web platform, evaluating 55 success criteria and identifying critical accessibility issues.

Key Insight: Many accessibility barriers came from structure, labeling, and responsive behavior, not just color contrast or obvious missing text.

Challenges
  • Missing or inconsistent alt text for images and functional graphics
  • Reflow and zoom issues causing horizontal scrolling at higher magnification
  • Unclear link purpose and inconsistent labeling across controls
  • ARIA and semantic HTML mismatches affecting screen reader output
Approach
  • Used DHS Trusted Tester methodology to evaluate compliance systematically
  • Tested with NVDA, keyboard navigation, ANDI, and IBM Accessibility Checker
  • Reviewed structure, labels, reading order, and responsive behavior
  • Documented findings and recommendations for the development team
Sample Findings
  • Missing alt text: Images were hidden or lacked meaningful descriptions.
  • Reflow issues: Content required horizontal scrolling at higher zoom levels.
  • Unclear link purpose: Links and controls were inconsistently labeled.

Outcome: Provided detailed remediation guidance and collaborated with developers to improve accessibility and awareness across the platform.

Accessible Document Remediation

PDF/UA • Section 508 • Manual tag tree auditing

Remediated a complex, multi-table PDF document to meet Section 508 and PDF/UA accessibility standards, ensuring it was usable with screen readers and other assistive technologies.

Key Insight: Document accessibility depends on structure, reading order, and table semantics, not just visual presentation.

Benchmark Summer Magazine Letter (PDF)↗ (opens in new tab)

Challenges
  • Complex multi-table layouts with nested structure
  • Correct table header scope and associations
  • Logical reading order across the document
  • Distinguishing meaningful content from decorative artifacts
Solutions
  • Performed manual tag tree audits to verify document structure
  • Corrected table header scope to support accurate navigation
  • Reviewed and adjusted reading order for screen reader usability
  • Managed artifact tagging for decorative elements
  • Validated the document against Section 508 and PDF/UA standards

Outcome: Delivered a remediated document with improved accessibility for screen reader users, more reliable table navigation, and clearer structural relationships for assistive technology.
